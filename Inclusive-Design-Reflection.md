# Inclusive Design Reflection

## Research notes

Vulnerable populations can include people who may face extra barriers, risk, or disadvantage when using digital products. In this context, that includes neurodivergent users such as people with ADHD or Autism, as well as people dealing with anxiety, burnout, disability, or other challenges that affect focus, routine, communication, or access.

In digital spaces, some common challenges could include confusing navigation, too many steps, unclear instructions, overwhelming notifications, sensory overload, or content that assumes everyone processes information the same way. Something that seems simple to one user might feel stressful or difficult to another user.

When designing or working with neurodivergent users, I think the main ethical consideration is to avoid making assumptions. Not everyone with ADHD or Autism has the same needs. It is important to use clear communication, give users control where possible, avoid shame-based language, and design in a way that supports people rather than making them feel judged.

Interactions and content can be made more accessible by using simple language, clear steps, predictable layouts, and reminders that are helpful rather than overwhelming. Reducing cognitive load is important. Users should not have to guess what to do next.

In a professional setting, supporting neurodivergent team members means being clear with expectations, allowing different working styles where possible, and not assuming that everyone communicates or works in the same way. Written instructions, clear priorities, and flexibility can make a big difference.

## Reflection

I can adjust my communication style by being more clear, patient, and direct. Instead of vague messages, I can explain what I mean, include enough context, and avoid rushing people for responses unless something is urgent.

A common UX or communication pitfall would be giving users too much information at once. Another one would be using unclear labels, unexpected changes, or notifications that interrupt users too often. For an app like Focus Bear, this matters because many users may already be dealing with focus issues or overwhelm.

One practical change I can make in my work is to write documentation and updates in a simple, structured way. For example, using short sections, clear headings, and direct next steps instead of long paragraphs. This would help both users and teammates understand information more easily.

## First-person account takeaway

One theme I noticed from first-person accounts of people with ADHD or Autism is that productivity tools can sometimes create extra pressure instead of helping. If the tool feels too strict, too complicated, or too judgemental, it can make the user feel worse when they fall behind.

A useful tool should make it easier to restart after getting distracted, not make the user feel like they failed. This stood out to me because Focus Bear is trying to support people with focus and routine, so the tone of the product matters as much as the features.

## Design or communication improvement

One improvement that could make Focus Bear more accessible is giving users more control over how reminders are shown.

For example, some users may prefer gentle reminders, while others may need stronger prompts. Some may want fewer notifications because too many could become overwhelming. Allowing users to adjust reminder tone, frequency, and intensity could make the app feel more supportive for different users.

## Supportive response example

Hi, thanks for sharing that. I’m sorry you’ve been finding it difficult to stay on track.

It’s completely okay to struggle with routines sometimes, especially when there are a lot of tasks or distractions. You don’t need to fix everything at once. A good first step could be choosing one small routine or focus session and making that easier to complete.

You could also try adjusting your reminders or breaking the task into smaller steps so it feels less overwhelming. If something in the app feels confusing or stressful, please let us know so we can understand what is not working well and improve it.

The goal is to support you, not make you feel pressured.
